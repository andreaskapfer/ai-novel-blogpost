### Prose Style Directive for *Wo die Schienen enden*  

#### **Sentence Rhythm & Cadence**  
- **Flow:** Reflect the locomotive’s rhythm—alternate between long, chugging sentences (for pastoral scenes/contemplation) and sharp, staccato bursts (during conflict/action).  
  - *Example:* "The train hissed like a living thing, its wheels biting the tracks with metallic teeth as dawn bled over the fields" (lyrical) vs. "Rifles cracked. Smoke swallowed the platform" (fragmented).  
- **Evolution:** Progressively tighten syntax from Act 1 (fluid, compound sentences) to Act 3 (shorter, punchier phrasing) to mirror societal fracture.  

#### **Paragraph Structure**  
- **Early Acts (1–4):** Dense, immersive paragraphs blending landscape description with character introspection. Use nested clauses to evoke interconnected village life.  
- **War Chapters (5–11):** Fragment paragraphs abruptly. Isolate single-sentence moments (e.g., "The bread vanished overnight") to emphasize dislocation.  
- **Final Chapter:** Return to measured, cohesive paragraphs—signifying reconstruction.  

#### **Descriptive Density**  
- **Settings:** Anchor scenes with tactile specificity—rust on rail joints, the sourdough tang of bakeries, creosote-soaked railroad ties. Layer sensory details (olfactory > auditory > visual) during tense moments (e.g., bombings).  
- **Characters:** Limit physical descriptions to functional, recurring traits (e.g., Karl’s grease-stained hands; Friedrich’s spectacles refracting light). Prioritize *embodied* perspective: a farmer views trains as "steel invaders," a teacher as "paperweight conquerors."  
- **Railway Motif:** Recurring focus on textures—icy rails, sweat-slicked locomotive controls, ash-coated sleepers—to reinforce thematic duality (progress/corrosion).  

#### **Metaphor & Symbolism**  
- **Frequency:** 1–2 vivid metaphors per page, escalating to 3–4 during climactic moments (e.g., bombings, deportations). Base comparisons on rural/industrial juxtaposition:  
  - *Examples:* "Nazi banners flapped like slaughterhouse flags"; "Friedrich’s diary entries were ghost stations—unused but still standing."  
- **Avoid:** Clichéd train metaphors ("life’s journey"). Instead, highlight mechanics: switches (moral choices), steam (suppressed rage), tracks (inescapable fate).  

#### **Tense & POV Conventions**  
- **Third-Limited:** Stick rigidly to one perspective per chapter. Use focalization to betray biases:  
  - Johann notes "wasted" land near tracks; Karl sees "geometric perfection" in the same space.  
- **Past Tense:** Maintain retrospective gravitas, but inject immediacy via:  
  - Inner monologue (*italics*): *Would the tracks outlast them all?*  
  - Sensory present tense: "The platform reeked of coal and fear *now*."  

#### **Narrative Voice**  
- **Baseline Tone:** Sober, chiaroscuro lyricism—equal parts elegy and document. Evoke Theodor Fontane’s social precision + Käthe Kollwitz’s visual starkness.  
- **Voice Shifts:**  
  - **Act 1:** Measured, faintly ironic ("Progress arrived wearing engineer’s boots").  
  - **Acts 2–3:** Shed ornamentation; adopt a witness’s clinical detachment ("The train car fit 52 men. They counted").  
  - **Epilogue:** Tentative warmth ("Rain pooled in bomb craters, reflecting a splintered sky").  
- **Dialogue Tags:** Sparingly used. Replace with action beats ("Elisabeth folded the deportation order. ‘They won’t take the children.’").  

#### **Key Stylistic Constraints**  
1. **Railway Lexicon:** Use technical terms metaphorically (*ballast*, *coupling*, *gauge*) but avoid jargon.  
2. **Silence Economy:** Minimize exposition. Imply WWII context through character ignorance (e.g., villagers debating "temporary" Nazi measures).  
3. **Foreshadowing:** Embed via:  
   - Weather patterns (unnaturally warm winters precede atrocities).  
   - Machinery sounds (locomotive whistles mimicking air raids).  

#### **Prohibitions**  
- No melodramatic adverbs ("sadly," "terribly").  
- No omniscient interjections.  
- No metaphors romanticizing war/oppression.
